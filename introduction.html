Introduction

April 9, 2024

I am a single unemployed American, 57 years old, currently in Cambodia, and I want to return to the United States, specifically Hawaii, because that’s the only place I am familiar with in America.

I was born in Japan and lived there until I was three years old. My family then emigrated to Hawaii, which had become a part of the United States roughly 15 years before we moved there.

I’ve spent more than 25 years in Asia. I first arrived in Thailand when I was about 32 years old, stayed there for about 20 years, then moved to Vietnam and lived there for about five years. I’ve also spent some time in Cambodia, where I am now. In about a week, I’ll be heading back to Vietnam because there’s a lot of short-term work there.

I am an English teacher in Asia. That’s what I’ve been doing for the past 25 years or so. I can’t say that I love it, and I can’t say that I hate it, but what I can say is that I feel old—too old to be a teacher at my age.

Most of my peers are in their 20s or 30s in Vietnam, but in Cambodia, it’s a little different; I notice that the teachers here are a bit older, maybe an average age in the mid-30s, and there are even teachers older than I am, which is why I chose to come here. However, it’s not a safe place for someone like me, a recovering alcoholic/drug addict.

It has also come to my attention that I am an autistic, self-diagnosed. There have been a few professionals in my life who have implied that I have autistic characteristics, but I buried those feelings for a long time until they resurfaced a few days ago.

I turned down a job in Cambodia. It was the best-paying job I’ve had yet in Asia, with pay at $26 to $32 an hour, and I was supposed to work 22 hours a week. I attended the induction and stayed the full 8 hours until the end of the day when we received our schedule.

The writings in this book are designed to tell the story of me, to try to get to the truth, the core of what really happened. It’s a painful story of my life. As difficult as it may sound, I have to go through it; it’s part of my recovery process.
